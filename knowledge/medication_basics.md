# Medication and Preventative Care for Pets

## Dogs — Preventative Medications

- **Flea and tick prevention**: Monthly topical treatments (Frontline, Advantage) or oral medications (NexGard, Bravecto). Year-round protection is recommended in most climates.
- **Heartworm prevention**: Monthly oral or topical medication (Heartgard, Interceptor). Heartworm disease is transmitted by mosquitoes and is potentially fatal. Annual heartworm testing is recommended.
- **Deworming**: Puppies should be dewormed every 2 weeks until 12 weeks old, then monthly until 6 months. Adult dogs should be dewormed 1-4 times per year depending on risk factors.
- **Joint supplements**: Glucosamine and chondroitin supplements are recommended for large breeds and senior dogs to support joint health.

## Cats — Preventative Medications

- **Flea prevention**: Monthly topical treatments (Revolution, Advantage) or oral medications. Even indoor-only cats can get fleas.
- **Heartworm prevention**: Monthly preventative recommended, especially in mosquito-heavy areas. There is no approved treatment for heartworm in cats — prevention is the only option.
- **Deworming**: Kittens should be dewormed at 2, 4, 6, and 8 weeks of age. Adult cats should be dewormed 1-4 times per year.

## Birds — Health Preventatives

- Birds do not typically require monthly preventative medications, but annual wellness exams with an avian vet are critical.
- **Vitamin supplements**: Vitamin A and calcium supplements may be needed if the diet is seed-heavy rather than pellet-based.
- **Air quality**: Birds are extremely sensitive to airborne toxins. Avoid Teflon/PTFE cookware fumes, scented candles, aerosol sprays, and cigarette smoke near birds.

## Administering Medication

- **Pills**: Hide in a treat, pill pocket, or use a pill dispenser. For cats, a pill gun can help.
- **Liquid medication**: Use a syringe to squirt into the side of the mouth. Go slowly to avoid aspiration.
- **Topical medication**: Part the fur and apply directly to the skin between the shoulder blades (where the pet cannot lick).
- **Never give human medication** to pets without veterinary approval — common human drugs like ibuprofen, acetaminophen, and aspirin can be fatal to pets.

## Signs Your Pet Needs Veterinary Attention

- Lethargy lasting more than 24 hours.
- Refusal to eat or drink for more than a day.
- Vomiting or diarrhea that persists beyond 24 hours.
- Difficulty breathing, coughing, or wheezing.
- Sudden changes in behavior, balance, or coordination.
- Visible pain, limping, or swelling.
- Blood in urine, stool, or vomit.
